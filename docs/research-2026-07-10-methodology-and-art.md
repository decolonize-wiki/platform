## 1. Candidate new categories (ranked)

### 1.1 Ethnographic present / temporal freezing — strongest candidate
Describing colonized peoples' abandoned practices in the present tense, or
representing them as existing only in the past ("stuck in time"). Fabian's
"denial of coevalness" — the Other represented as "always out of time."
- **[verified]** Words Matter explicitly cites Fabian's "ethnographic
  present" as a text-checkable pattern (present-tense description of
  abandoned practices, diminutives) — museum-practice citation for a
  verb-tense-level criterion.
- **[verified]** Warsame's essay in Words Matter (pp. 79–85) names "Stuck in
  time and space" as trope 1 of five colonial mechanisms.
- Text-checkable: verb tense is mechanically detectable; criteria could be
  "a practice documented as historical is described in the article's own
  voice in the present tense."
- Source to add: Johannes Fabian, *Time and the Other: How Anthropology
  Makes Its Object*, Columbia UP, 1983; plus Words Matter (modest-lelijveld-2018).

### 1.2 Homogenization / erasure of specific nationhood
Catch-all labels ("Native American group", "African tribe") where the
specific nation/people is known — flattening distinct nations into an
undifferentiated mass.
- **[verified]** NAJA/IJA guide: failing to name the specific tribe "is
  neither accurate, fair or thorough and undermines diversity by erasing the
  tribe's identity. The practice never happens when reporting on other
  places or groups." Incorrect/correct pairs given ("Native American Tribe
  To Open First Marijuana Resort" vs "Santee Sioux Tribe...").
- **[unverified]** IJA flags homogenization as a reporting failure none of
  our six categories captures; Native Governance Center guide sets the same
  specificity rule.
- Text-checkable: generic label present + specific nation attested in
  scholarly record + never named in article.
- Note: overlaps with the already-promoted `delegitimizing-labels` candidate
  only partially — that one is about asymmetric hostile labels, this about
  generic erasure. Keep separate.

### 1.3 Possessive / jurisdictional subordination
Possessive constructions framing peoples as belonging to settler states:
"Wyoming's Indigenous people", "Brazil's Indians".
- **[verified]** NAJA: avoid possessives framing Indigenous people as
  "possessions of states or countries"; rewrite "the Indigenous people of
  Wyoming".
- **[unverified]** Native Governance Center prescribes "share geography
  with" as the rewrite (verification votes all errored on rate limit —
  source PDF is fetched and quotable, re-verify later).
- Text-checkable: possessive construction + Indigenous/colonized people as
  the possessed noun.
- Caution for hostile-historian test: possessives are ubiquitous and often
  innocuous ("Brazil's population"); criteria must be narrow — the possessed
  noun must be a people/nation, not a demographic statistic.

### 1.4 Civilizational hierarchy / progress metanarrative
Article structure or vocabulary implying a savage→civilized trajectory, or
colonial extraction framed as "progress"/"development".
- **[unverified]** Bjork-James (2021) cites Segal (2000): encyclopedic/
  textbook structure still carries the "long chronology" social-evolutionary
  metanarrative anthropology overturned (Boas 1920).
- **[verified]** Words Matter documents "progress" used for colonial
  industries in the Dutch East Indies as a flagged pattern.
- Harder to make text-checkable at passage level (often structural), but
  vocabulary criteria are feasible: "civilized/civilizing", "advanced/
  backward", "progress" applied to extraction economies in the article's
  own voice.
- Sources: Bjork-James 2021 (below); Daniel Segal, "'Western Civ' and the
  Staging of History in American Higher Education", AHR 105(3), 2000.

### 1.5 Exotification / othering (weaker — hard to make checkable)
- **[verified]** Warsame trope 2: "Exotification, 'Othering' and the
  assumption of whiteness."
- Difficult to reduce to defensible passage-level criteria; risks failing
  the hostile-historian test. Park as CANDIDATES.md note, don't draft yet.

### 1.6 Out of scope but recorded: cartographic terra nullius
- **[unverified]** Bjork-James documents the English Wikipedia "French and
  Indian War" map rendering Native-held North America as empty gray space.
  Our methodology is text-only today; note for a future images/maps scope
  decision.

## 3. Decolonial art

### Movements / visual language (workstream 3a)
- **OSPAAAL / Tricontinental posters** (Havana, 1966–2019) — the strongest
  aesthetic match for "Protest Press, evolved": bold flat color,
  photomontage, direct visual metaphor with pop-art influence (curator
  Olivia Ahmed) **[unverified]**, explicitly global-decolonization scope
  (multilingual editions ES/EN/FR/AR, distribution across 87 countries)
  **[unverified]**. Named artists: Alfredo Rostgaard (artistic director
  1966–75; "Create two, three... many Vietnams", 1967), René Mederos,
  Olivio Martínez, Faustino Pérez, Rafael Morante; documented aesthetic
  exchange with Black Panther artist Emory Douglas **[unverified]**.
- **Antropofagia / Tarsila do Amaral** — scholarly grounding exists for it
  as early decolonial visual thought (devouring the colonizer)
  **[unverified]**, but the same scholarship flags the appropriation
  critique: Tarsila's white upper-class position and the "trap of
  exoticism" — a real caveat for a site centering Black/Afro-descendent
  people. Use as intellectual reference, not primary visual language.
- Site is already aesthetically adjacent to OSPAAAL (paper/ink/red, mono
  stamps); the research supports deepening that lineage rather than
  importing artworks.

### Practical acquisition paths (workstream 3b)
